The honest truth

Does a degree actually
matter here?

MLOps sits at the intersection of DevOps and machine learning. Companies hiring MLOps engineers care about whether you can build reliable ML pipelines, manage model versioning, set up monitoring, and keep production systems stable. These are demonstrated skills — not academic credentials.

The shortage of MLOps engineers is acute. Most ML teams have brilliant data scientists who can train models and no one who knows how to deploy them reliably. Companies are actively looking for people who can bridge that gap, and they're not filtering by degree when they find someone who can.

What actually gets you hired

A GitHub repo showing an end-to-end ML pipeline in production — data ingestion, training, versioning with MLflow or DVC, deployment, and monitoring with drift detection. Most candidates applying for MLOps roles have none of this. One working production pipeline built without a job title puts you ahead of the majority of applicants.

Where degree matters less

Tech companies and AI-first startups

Companies with mature ML practices evaluate MLOps candidates on technical interviews and portfolio evidence. A demonstrated ability to build and operate ML infrastructure is what matters — your university is irrelevant to that assessment.

Where degree matters more

Large enterprise and government

Government contractors and large enterprises with rigid HR screening may require formal qualifications. These roles typically move slowly and pay less — self-taught MLOps engineers with strong portfolios tend to target faster-moving environments.

Myth vs reality

What people get
wrong.

Myth
You need to understand how to train ML models to do MLOps.
Reality
MLOps is about operationalising models that data scientists have already trained. You need to understand what models need — data, compute, versioning, monitoring — more than how to build them from scratch. Think of it like being a DevOps engineer for ML systems.
Myth
MLOps is just DevOps with a different name.
Reality
DevOps skills are the foundation, but ML introduces unique challenges: data versioning, model drift, training pipelines, feature stores, experiment tracking. Software doesn't drift silently in production the way a machine learning model can. MLOps addresses problems that standard DevOps tooling wasn't designed for.
Myth
You need a data science background to get into MLOps.
Reality
DevOps and backend engineers make excellent MLOps engineers because they already understand production systems, reliability, and automation. The ML concepts you need are learnable. The infrastructure thinking that data scientists often lack is much harder to build from scratch.
Myth
The MLOps tooling is too complex to learn without formal training.
Reality
MLflow, Kubeflow, and Weights & Biases all have extensive free documentation and tutorials. The tooling is maturing rapidly and becoming more accessible. The concepts behind the tools — versioning, monitoring, automation — are the same ones you apply in standard software engineering.

The path

How to get hired
without a degree.

1
Month 1–2

Learn enough ML to understand what you're operationalising

You need to understand the ML workflow — training, validation, hyperparameter tuning, model evaluation — well enough to build infrastructure around it. Fast.ai's Practical Deep Learning is the best practical introduction. Andrew Ng's Machine Learning Specialisation covers fundamentals. The goal is fluency, not expertise.

2
Month 2–4

Build your first end-to-end ML pipeline

Take a simple ML project — churn prediction, image classification, sentiment analysis — and operationalise it properly. Use MLflow for experiment tracking. DVC for data versioning. Package it in Docker. Deploy it. Set up basic monitoring. This single project, done properly and put on GitHub, demonstrates more than any certification.

3
Month 3–6

Master orchestration and feature stores

Real MLOps at scale requires pipeline orchestration. Learn Apache Airflow or Kubeflow Pipelines — understand DAGs, dependency management, and scheduled retraining. Then explore feature stores, which is where most companies struggle: serving consistent features between training and production without data leakage.

4
Month 5–9

Learn model monitoring and drift detection

This is the most underrated MLOps skill — and the one most junior practitioners skip. Models degrade silently. Learn how to detect data drift (when your input distribution shifts) and concept drift (when the relationship between inputs and outputs changes). Tools: Evidently AI, WhyLogs, Arize. This skill gets you hired at companies with mature ML practices.

5
Month 8–18

Target companies with existing ML in production

The best MLOps roles are at companies that already have models in production and are struggling to maintain them — not companies experimenting with AI for the first time. Look for job descriptions that mention specific tools (MLflow, Kubeflow, SageMaker) rather than generic "AI experience." Those are companies with real ML infrastructure that need real MLOps engineers.

FAQ

Common questions.

Data Engineers build pipelines that move and transform data for analytics and reporting. MLOps engineers build pipelines specifically for training, deploying, and monitoring machine learning models. There's overlap — both work with data infrastructure — but the ML-specific concerns of MLOps (experiment tracking, model versioning, drift detection, model serving) are distinct from standard data engineering work.
Kubernetes knowledge is increasingly expected for senior MLOps roles, particularly at companies running ML workloads at scale. For entry-level positions, Docker fluency and basic Kubernetes understanding is usually sufficient. Focus on getting the ML-specific tooling right first — Kubeflow runs on Kubernetes, so you'll learn it naturally as you go deeper.
AWS SageMaker is the most widely used managed MLOps platform and knowing it makes you hireable at the most companies. Google Vertex AI is the second most common. Azure ML is relevant if you're targeting Microsoft-heavy enterprises. The concepts transfer between platforms, so going deep on one is better than surface knowledge of all three. AWS is the safest bet for maximum optionality.
MLOps engineers command strong salaries because qualified candidates are scarce. Entry level with a DevOps background transitioning to MLOps: $110k–$145k. Mid-level with 2–3 years MLOps experience: $145k–$190k. Senior MLOps engineers at tech companies: $190k–$260k+. The scarcity premium is real — companies with production ML are genuinely struggling to hire people who can keep it running reliably.
Almost everything you know transfers. CI/CD pipelines become ML training pipelines. Container orchestration becomes model serving infrastructure. Monitoring and alerting become model performance monitoring. Infrastructure as code applies directly. The ML-specific additions — experiment tracking, model registry, feature stores, drift detection — build on top of the infrastructure thinking you've already developed. Your gap is smaller than you think.
