The honest truth

Does a degree actually
matter here?

Product Design sits at a specific intersection that design degrees rarely prepare people for well — the combination of user research, interaction design, visual craft, and product thinking that working in a tech company actually requires. Many design school graduates are strong visually but weak on user research and product strategy. Many career changers from customer-facing, analytical, or writing backgrounds are strong on research and thinking but need to build the visual and tool skills.

Tech companies hiring product designers care primarily about portfolio quality and the ability to discuss design decisions clearly in interviews. Whether you developed those skills at art school, in a bootcamp, or through deliberate self-study is largely irrelevant to the hiring decision.

What actually gets you hired

A portfolio site with 2–3 case studies each showing the complete design process: problem framing, user research, how research shaped decisions, design iterations, usability testing, and the final shipped or prototyped solution. The ability to explain what you would do differently is as important as showing what you did. Hiring managers are evaluating your thinking, not just your Figma skills.

Where degree matters less

Product companies and startups

Tech companies from Series A startups to publicly listed product companies evaluate designers on portfolio quality, culture fit, and the ability to articulate design decisions. Graphic designers, researchers, marketers, and engineers all successfully transition into product design at these companies.

Where degree matters more

Brand and agency design roles

Traditional advertising agencies and brand design studios are more likely to value formal design education — particularly for roles with heavy graphic design components. Product design at tech companies is a more accessible target for self-taught practitioners.

Myth vs reality

What people get
wrong.

Myth
Product Design and UX Design are the same role.
Reality
Product Designers typically own the full design of a product or feature — from research and information architecture through interaction design and visual polish, often including some product strategy involvement. UX Designers often focus more narrowly on research and interaction patterns. At smaller companies the roles are identical; at larger companies they're distinct with different scope.
Myth
You need exceptional visual design talent to become a product designer.
Reality
Visual craft matters and you need to reach a professional standard — but hiring decisions are more often made on the quality of your thinking than your visual execution. Designers who understand user problems deeply and make well-reasoned decisions consistently get promoted over those who produce beautiful work that doesn't solve the right problem.
Myth
Figma is just for mockups — any design tool will do.
Reality
Figma is the industry standard at virtually every tech company — for wireframing, high-fidelity design, prototyping, design systems, and collaboration with engineering. Knowing Figma deeply — components, auto layout, variables, prototyping — isn't just useful, it's expected. Most design tool questions in interviews are Figma-specific.
Myth
Your previous career is a disadvantage — you're starting from scratch.
Reality
A former nurse designing healthcare products, a former teacher designing education tools, or a former operations manager designing workflow software understands the user in ways that design school graduates genuinely don't. Domain expertise makes your research more insightful and your design decisions more informed. It's an advantage, not a liability.

The path

How to get hired
without a degree.

1
Month 1–2

Get fluent in Figma and design fundamentals

Figma's own free learning platform covers the tool comprehensively. Supplement with Refactoring UI by Adam Wathan and Steve Schoger — it teaches visual design fundamentals specifically for people who aren't visual designers by training. The goal in month one is being able to produce professional-quality high-fidelity designs, not just wireframes. Visual polish matters at the portfolio stage.

2
Month 2–4

Run 5 real user interviews

Pick a product in your previous industry that frustrates people. Recruit 5 users. Conduct structured interviews — not surveys, not "what do you want," but observation-based conversations about how they currently do something and where it breaks. Synthesise what you learn using affinity mapping. This is the skill that separates product designers from people who just make things look nice — and the one most hiring managers probe hardest in interviews.

3
Month 3–6

Build 3 complete case studies

Each case study needs: a real problem (not a redesign of Spotify's onboarding), user research findings that shaped your decisions, wireframes showing your thinking process, high-fidelity designs in Figma, a prototype, usability testing with at least 3 people, and iterations you made based on what you found. The case study write-up is as important as the designs — it's how you show your thinking to hiring managers who can't interview you yet.

4
Month 5–8

Learn design systems and component thinking

Senior designers and design-mature companies expect you to think in components — reusable, consistent patterns rather than one-off designs. Build a small design system in Figma for one of your portfolio projects — define your colour tokens, typography scale, spacing system, and core components. This demonstrates production-readiness that many junior designer portfolios lack.

5
Month 7–15

Target your previous domain and get portfolio feedback first

Before applying broadly, share your portfolio in designer communities — ADPList, Designer Hangout, Dribbble. Get feedback from working product designers. Most self-taught designers apply too early with portfolios that aren't yet competitive. The difference between a portfolio that gets callbacks and one that doesn't is often subtle — experienced designers can spot it immediately and tell you what to fix before you waste applications.

FAQ

Common questions.

Target "Product Designer" at tech companies — it's the more comprehensive role and typically better compensated. "UX Designer" titles are more common at agencies, enterprise software companies, and non-tech organisations. If you're targeting startups and product companies, Product Designer is the right target. The skills overlap significantly — build toward the broader Product Designer role from the start.
Basic HTML and CSS literacy is increasingly valuable — understanding how your designs get implemented helps you make better decisions and communicate more effectively with engineers. You don't need to build production features. But being able to inspect a component in browser DevTools, understand CSS properties, and have meaningful conversations about implementation complexity makes you a significantly better collaborator and a more hireable designer.
Dribbble is useful for visibility and community — not as a hiring tool for product design. Most Dribbble work shows polished visual outputs without the research and decision-making process that product design hiring is actually evaluating. A well-documented case study on your own portfolio site is far more effective than a Dribbble profile full of beautiful shots with no context. Build the portfolio site. Dribbble is secondary.

Product design salaries at top tech companies are competitive with engineering salaries at comparable seniority levels. The ceiling is higher than most career changers expect going in.
