The honest truth

Does a degree actually
matter here?

AI Governance is one of the only emerging tech roles where non-technical backgrounds have a structural advantage. The EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, and ISO 42001 are regulatory and risk frameworks — they require legal reading, policy writing, audit thinking, and stakeholder management. Not algorithms.

The shortage isn't technical people who learned governance. It's governance professionals who learned enough AI to apply their existing skills. If you've spent time in compliance, risk, legal, or policy — you're already halfway there.

What actually gets you hired

Evidence that you understand both the regulatory landscape (EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF) and how AI systems actually work at a conceptual level. Hiring managers are looking for people who can sit between the technical team and the board — translating risk in both directions. A degree in computer science does not teach you that.

Where degree matters less

Regulated industries moving fast

Financial services, healthcare, and insurance companies need AI governance practitioners urgently. They care about regulatory knowledge, risk frameworks, and audit experience — none of which require a specific degree.

Where degree matters more

Academic and research roles

University AI ethics research positions and some government policy roles may prefer formal academic credentials. These are a small fraction of the total market for AI governance skills.

Myth vs reality

What people get
wrong.

Myth
You need a technical background to work in AI governance.
Reality
You need enough AI literacy to understand how models fail, what bias looks like, and how to evaluate vendor claims. That's learnable in weeks. The governance, risk, and compliance skills take years — and you may already have them.
Myth
AI governance is just ethics — too soft to be a real career.
Reality
The EU AI Act creates mandatory compliance requirements with fines up to 6% of global revenue. AI governance is now a legal and financial risk management function. Companies are hiring for it urgently and paying accordingly.
Myth
The frameworks are too new — there's nothing to learn yet.
Reality
The NIST AI RMF launched in 2023. ISO 42001 published in 2023. The EU AI Act is in force. The AIGP certification exists. There is a rapidly maturing body of knowledge — and the people who learn it now will be the senior practitioners in two years.
Myth
My GRC or legal background isn't relevant to AI.
Reality
Risk assessment, control frameworks, audit methodology, policy writing, vendor due diligence — these map directly to AI governance work. Your existing skills are the hardest part to learn. The AI-specific layer is the easier addition.

The path

How to get hired
without a degree.

1
Month 1–2

Learn how AI systems actually work

You don't need to become a data scientist. You need to understand how models are trained, where they fail, what bias looks like technically, and what "hallucination" actually means. Andrew Ng's AI for Everyone course is the right level. The goal is enough depth to have credible conversations with technical teams.

2
Month 2–4

Read the actual frameworks

Read the NIST AI RMF cover to cover. Work through the EU AI Act's key articles — particularly the risk classification tiers and prohibited practices. Study ISO 42001. These aren't light reading but they're exactly what you'll be applying daily. Start mapping them to risk frameworks you already know.

3
Month 3–6

Pursue the AIGP certification

The IAPP's AI Governance Professional (AIGP) certification is the emerging standard for this role. It validates your knowledge of AI governance frameworks, risk management, and regulatory requirements. It signals seriousness to hiring managers in a way that a self-study claim cannot.

4
Month 4–8

Build an AI governance artefact

Write an AI risk assessment for a publicly available AI system. Draft an AI use policy for a hypothetical organisation. Create a vendor AI due diligence checklist. These demonstrate applied thinking, not just knowledge. Publish them. This is your portfolio.

5
Month 6–14

Target regulated industries first

Financial services, healthcare, insurance, and legal are under the most immediate regulatory pressure to demonstrate AI governance. They have the budget, the urgency, and the existing GRC infrastructure that your background fits into. Large consultancies (Deloitte, KPMG, PwC) also have active AI governance practices hiring now.
