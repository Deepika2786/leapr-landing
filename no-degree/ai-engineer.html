The honest truth

Does a degree actually
matter here?

AI Engineering as a defined role didn't exist in 2022. The tools — LangChain, LlamaIndex, vector databases, RAG pipelines — are all newer than most university curricula. Universities are still writing the syllabus for what practitioners have already been doing for two years.

This is one of the only fields where self-taught practitioners are genuinely ahead of formally educated ones. The people hiring AI Engineers care about one thing: can you build something that works in production.

What actually gets you hired

A GitHub repo with a deployed AI application. The ability to explain how you chose your embedding model, why you structured your RAG pipeline the way you did, and what broke in production. No hiring manager has ever asked an AI Engineer for a transcript.

Where degree matters less

AI-first startups and scaleups

Companies building with LLMs, agents, and AI-native products hire entirely on demonstrated ability. Your GitHub is your CV. They move too fast to care about credentials.

Where degree matters more

Enterprise compliance roles

Large banks, government contractors, and regulated industries may require formal qualifications for compliance reasons — but these represent a small and shrinking share of AI engineering roles.

Myth vs reality

What people get
wrong.

Myth
You need to understand the math behind transformers to be an AI Engineer.
Reality
AI Engineers work at the application layer — APIs, pipelines, retrieval systems. You need to know how to use models effectively, not derive backpropagation from scratch.
Myth
You need to train your own models.
Reality
95% of AI Engineering jobs use existing foundation models via API. Your job is orchestration, retrieval, evaluation, and deployment — not training from scratch.
Myth
You need a CS degree to be taken seriously.
Reality
The field is so new that "formal training" in AI Engineering simply doesn't exist yet. Hiring managers evaluate GitHub, deployed projects, and technical interviews — not degrees.
Myth
You need to know ML before becoming an AI Engineer.
Reality
AI Engineering and ML Engineering are different roles. AI Engineers build products on top of models. ML Engineers train and optimise them. You can be excellent at one without deep knowledge of the other.

The path

How to get hired
without a degree.

1
Month 1–2

Master the LLM API layer

Get fluent with the OpenAI and Anthropic APIs. Understand tokens, context windows, system prompts, and temperature. Build 3–4 small projects — a document Q&A bot, a classification pipeline, a summarisation tool. These aren't portfolio pieces yet, just reps.

2
Month 2–4

Build a real RAG application

RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) is the core pattern behind most AI products. Pick a domain you understand — legal docs, medical records, internal wikis — and build a RAG pipeline from scratch using LlamaIndex or LangChain with a vector database. Deploy it. This is your first real portfolio piece.

3
Month 3–6

Learn evaluation and observability

Production AI is broken without evaluation. Learn LangSmith or Weights & Biases for tracing. Understand hallucination detection, retrieval quality metrics, and how to build eval datasets. Engineers who can measure quality get hired over those who can only build.

4
Month 5–9

Build an AI agent

Agents are where the field is heading. Build a tool-using agent — something that can search the web, query a database, and take actions based on LLM reasoning. Use LangGraph or AutoGen. Agents that actually work in production are rare and extremely hireable.

5
Month 8–18

Target AI-first companies specifically

Apply to companies where AI is the product, not a feature — not companies "exploring AI." Your GitHub should show deployed applications, not just notebooks. Write about what you built and what broke. Engineers who communicate their process clearly get offers over silent coders.
