The honest truth

Does a degree actually
matter here?

Cybersecurity has a well-established certification pathway that most hiring managers actively prefer over academic degrees. CompTIA Security+, CEH, and CISSP are industry-standard credentials that signal real, current competency — not theoretical knowledge from a textbook published three years ago.

There are currently over 750,000 unfilled cybersecurity jobs in the US alone. The talent shortage is so acute that employers have systematically loosened degree requirements to fill roles. What they haven't loosened is the expectation for hands-on technical skills and verified certifications.

What actually gets you hired

A combination of CompTIA Security+ certification, hands-on lab experience (TryHackMe, HackTheBox), and a home lab you can talk about in interviews. Being able to walk through a real incident response scenario — what you'd look for, what tools you'd use, how you'd contain and document it — matters far more than any transcript.

Where degree matters less

MSPs, startups, and mid-market

Managed security service providers and growth-stage companies hire almost entirely on certifications and demonstrated skills. These are also where the most interesting hands-on work happens — you're not waiting two years to touch a real incident.

Where degree matters more

Federal government and defence

US federal agencies and defence contractors often require degrees for certain clearance levels and position classifications. These roles also move slowly. If government is your target, a degree becomes more relevant — but it's still not the only path.

Myth vs reality

What people get
wrong.

Myth
You need to know how to hack to work in cybersecurity.
Reality
Most cybersecurity analyst roles focus on defence — monitoring SIEM alerts, investigating incidents, managing vulnerabilities, writing policies. Offensive skills (penetration testing) are a specialist track, not the entry point. Start on the blue team.
Myth
You need a computer science background to break in.
Reality
Military veterans, IT support professionals, network administrators, and compliance officers all transition successfully into cybersecurity. The analytical thinking, attention to detail, and comfort with procedures you develop in non-technical roles are genuinely valuable in security work.
Myth
Cybersecurity is too technical to learn without formal training.
Reality
TryHackMe and HackTheBox have guided, structured learning paths that take you from zero to job-ready. These platforms are used by professional security teams for ongoing training. The learning infrastructure for self-taught cybersecurity is genuinely excellent.
Myth
The field is saturated — too many people are trying to get in.
Reality
The entry-level market is competitive but the qualified candidate shortage at mid-level and above is severe. Get through the first 12–18 months in any security role and you become genuinely scarce. The people who can't find work are the ones with only certifications and no hands-on lab experience to back them up.

The path

How to get hired
without a degree.

1
Month 1–3

Earn CompTIA Security+

This is the non-negotiable first step. Security+ is the baseline certification that gets you past HR screening at most companies. Study with Professor Messer's free course and the CompTIA CertMaster practice exams. Three months of focused preparation is enough for most people. This single certification unlocks a significant portion of entry-level job listings.

2
Month 2–5

Build hands-on skills on TryHackMe

Complete the SOC Level 1 learning path on TryHackMe. This covers SIEM fundamentals, incident response, threat intelligence, and network analysis — the actual day-to-day work of a security analyst. TryHackMe tracks your progress publicly, so you can link your profile in job applications as evidence of hands-on practice.

3
Month 3–6

Set up a home lab

Run a Splunk or Elastic SIEM on a virtual machine. Generate logs. Write detection rules. Simulate attacks using Atomic Red Team. Document what you built and what you learned. Being able to say "I built a SIEM at home and wrote 12 detection rules" in an interview is concrete evidence that sets you apart from candidates who only have certifications.

4
Month 5–9

Target SOC Analyst Tier 1 roles

Your first role will likely be a SOC Analyst Tier 1 — monitoring alerts, triaging incidents, escalating confirmed threats. These roles exist at MSSPs (managed security service providers), mid-sized companies, and large enterprises. The work can be repetitive, but the exposure to real incidents and tools is irreplaceable. Plan to spend 12–18 months here before moving up.

5
Month 12–24

Stack certifications strategically

After landing your first role, pursue CompTIA CySA+ (defensive analysis) or CEH (ethical hacking basics). If you're targeting cloud security, AWS Security Specialty or Microsoft SC-200 are highly valued. Each certification stacks on the last and opens the next tier of roles. CISSP is the senior practitioner certification — realistic after 3–5 years of experience.

FAQ

Common questions.

SOC (Security Operations Centre) Analyst is often the entry-level title — you're monitoring SIEM alerts, triaging incidents, and escalating confirmed threats. Cybersecurity Analyst is a broader title that can include vulnerability management, security policy, compliance, and incident response. Most people start in a SOC role and move into broader analyst work as they gain experience.
Basic scripting is increasingly useful — Python for automating repetitive tasks and parsing logs, Bash for Linux administration. You don't need to be a software developer. But being able to write a Python script that pulls logs from an API and formats them for analysis makes you significantly more effective than an analyst who can only click through a GUI.
At entry level, the market is competitive because many people are trying to break in with only certifications and no practical experience. At mid-level and above, the shortage is real and acute. The fastest path through the bottleneck: get Security+, build a home lab, complete the TryHackMe SOC path, and apply to MSSP roles. MSSPs are the most reliable entry point because they always have volume hiring needs.
SOC Analyst Tier 1: $55k–$80k. Mid-level Security Analyst: $85k–$120k. Senior Analyst or Security Engineer: $120k–$170k. Specialised roles (cloud security, penetration testing, threat intelligence) at senior levels: $150k–$220k+. Location matters significantly — remote security roles are common, which helps salaries in lower cost-of-living areas.
Start defensive (blue team). SOC analyst roles are more abundant at entry level, the path is clearer, and defensive experience actually makes you better at offensive work later. Penetration testing is a competitive specialty that usually requires 2–3 years of defensive experience first. The "I want to be a hacker" path that skips defensive fundamentals is longer and less reliable than it sounds.
