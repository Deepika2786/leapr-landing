Why you're not getting
interview calls.
The honest answer.

You're applying. You're qualified — or at least you think you are. Nobody's calling. This is not a confidence problem. It's an information problem. Here's what's actually happening.

5 minute read — worth it if you've been applying for more than 4 weeks with no response.

First — it's probably
not what you think.

Most people assume silence means rejection. It usually doesn't. It means your application never reached a human being.

In 2026, over 75% of applications are filtered out before a recruiter sees them — by ATS software, keyword matching algorithms, and automated screening tools. You could be genuinely excellent for the role and still get nothing back.

The other assumption people make is that they need more experience. Sometimes true. But more often, the problem is precision — you're applying to roles where there's a specific skill gap nobody told you about. Not a big gap. Not an insurmountable one. Just an unaddressed one.

The real reasons.
One by one.

01

Your resume has the wrong skills listed — not wrong experience, wrong skills.

This is the most common and least obvious problem. Job postings in 2026 are increasingly specific about the exact skills they want. ATS systems are trained to find those exact keywords. If you're a Product Manager who has done roadmap prioritisation but your resume says "strategic planning" — you're invisible to the filter looking for "roadmap" and "prioritisation".

The fix is not rewriting your resume. The fix is understanding exactly which skills your target role requires in 2026 — not which skills you have — and closing the gap between the two.

What to do

Take 3 job descriptions for your target role. Pull out every skill mentioned more than twice. Compare against your resume. The gap between those two lists is your priority list — not a long course, just targeted, specific additions.

02

You're applying to roles where you have a hidden skill gap.

This is different from the resume problem. Your resume might be perfectly fine — but you're genuinely missing one or two skills the role requires, and nobody told you. Not your last manager. Not the job description (which is often outdated). Not LinkedIn.

The market moved. What companies hired for in 2023 is not what they're hiring for in 2026. Roles that didn't require SQL now do. Roles that didn't expect AI fluency now do. If you're using a skills profile from 3 years ago — you have gaps you don't know about.

What to do

Get a current, specific gap analysis for your exact transition. Not generic career advice — a personalised map of what your target role actually requires in 2026 versus what your background gives you. Leapr does this. Upload your resume, tell it your target role, and it shows you the precise gap.

03

Your resume is being rejected by ATS before any human sees it.

ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems) are used by over 90% of large companies. They scan for keywords, format, and relevance scores. A beautifully designed resume with tables, columns, graphics, or unusual fonts often scores zero — not because the content is bad, but because the ATS can't parse it.

Common ATS killers: two-column layouts, headers and footers with important information, graphics or icons, unusual section titles ("Where I've Been" instead of "Experience"), and PDFs that weren't exported correctly.

What to do

Use a clean, single-column resume. Simple section headings. No tables. No graphics. Export as PDF from a standard word processor. The goal is machine-readable first, human-beautiful second.

04

You're applying too broadly instead of too specifically.

Sending 50 applications to 50 different roles feels productive. It usually isn't. When you apply broadly, your resume isn't tailored to any specific role — which means it passes fewer ATS filters and resonates with fewer hiring managers.

The counterintuitive truth: 10 highly targeted applications outperform 100 generic ones. Each application should be tailored to that specific role's keywords and requirements. This takes more time per application — but dramatically more time per interview.

What to do

Pick one specific target role and target company type. Tailor your resume specifically to that role's language. Apply to 10 well-chosen companies rather than 100 random ones. Track everything — role, company, date, response. Patterns will emerge.

05

Your LinkedIn profile doesn't match your resume — or doesn't exist properly.

Recruiters who see an interesting resume immediately check LinkedIn. If your LinkedIn is outdated, sparse, or tells a different story than your resume — they close the tab. You had them and lost them.

LinkedIn also has its own search algorithm. Recruiters search for candidates using keywords. If your profile doesn't contain the right keywords — especially your target role title, key skills, and industry terms — you won't appear in their searches at all.

What to do

Your LinkedIn headline should contain your target role title — not just your current one. Your About section should use the exact language of your target role. Your Skills section should list the specific skills that role requires. Make yourself findable, not just presentable.

06

You're applying through the front door when everyone else is too.

"Easy Apply" on LinkedIn and the "Submit" button on company career pages puts you in a pool of 200–500 applicants. Even if you're excellent, the odds are brutal at that volume. The people getting interviews from the same roles are often getting there a different way.

Referrals account for roughly 30–40% of hires at most companies — but only about 7% of applicants. The math is simple: referrals get a dramatically higher hit rate.

What to do

For every role you apply to, spend 10 minutes finding someone at that company on LinkedIn — especially in the team you'd join or someone with your target title. Send a short, specific message. Not asking for a job — asking for 15 minutes of their time to learn about the role. One warm referral is worth 50 cold applications.

07

The job market in your target role is genuinely harder right now.

This one is important to acknowledge honestly. Some roles have more applicants than openings in 2026. Entry-level software engineering, marketing generalist, and project management roles are all more competitive than they were two years ago.

This isn't a reason to give up — it's a reason to be more strategic about which roles you target. Adjacent roles, emerging specialisations, and industries with genuine talent shortages (healthcare tech, AI governance, cybersecurity, data engineering) have far better hit rates than saturated markets.

What to do

Research whether your target role has more supply than demand right now. If so, consider an adjacent role that uses your skills but has less competition. Getting in the door in a slightly different role than your dream role is often the fastest path to the dream role itself.
