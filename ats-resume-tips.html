What ATS actually
is.

ATS stands for Applicant Tracking System. It's software that companies use to manage job applications — and in 2026, over 90% of large companies and a growing number of smaller ones use it. When you click "Apply", your resume doesn't land in a recruiter's inbox. It lands in a database where it's parsed, scored, and ranked.

The ATS reads your resume like a machine — because it is one. It looks for specific keywords, checks that your experience matches minimum requirements, and scores your application against other applicants. If your score is too low, you're filtered out automatically. The recruiter never sees you.

What kills your
ATS score.

01

Two-column layouts

Visually impressive but ATS reads left-to-right, line by line. A two-column resume gets parsed as garbled text — your skills column gets mixed with your experience column. The machine reads nonsense and scores you low.

Fix

Single column only. Always. Every time.

02

Graphics, icons, and tables

ATS systems cannot read images or parse tables reliably. Skill bars showing "Python: 80%" — the ATS sees nothing. Icons next to your contact details — invisible. Any information in a graphic is information that doesn't exist to the machine.

Fix

Text only. Skills as a simple comma-separated list or bullet points. No visual elements that carry information.

03

Wrong keywords

ATS systems are trained on the job description. They look for exact or close matches to the keywords the hiring team specified. "Managed cross-functional teams" won't match a filter looking for "stakeholder management". Same concept, invisible to the machine.

Fix

Read the job description carefully. Use its exact language in your resume where it's truthful to do so. If they say "product roadmap" — say "product roadmap", not "product strategy".

04

Unusual section headings

ATS systems expect standard section names: Experience, Education, Skills, Summary. Creative alternatives like "Where I've Been", "What I Know", or "My Journey" confuse the parser — it doesn't know where to look for your experience.

Fix

Use standard section names. Boring works.

05

Headers and footers

Most ATS systems don't parse headers and footers reliably. If you put your name, contact info, or anything important in the header or footer — there's a real chance the ATS never sees it.

Fix

Put all important information in the main body of the document.

What actually
works.

The good news: ATS optimisation is not difficult once you understand the rules. It's mostly about removing things that hurt you and adding the right keywords.

The ATS-safe resume checklist

Single column layout · Standard section headings (Experience, Education, Skills) · No graphics, icons, tables, or images · No headers or footers · Clean font (Arial, Calibri, Georgia) · 10-12pt body text · Exported as a clean PDF or .docx · Contact details in the main body · Keywords from the job description used naturally throughout

Beyond formatting — the most powerful thing you can do is keyword match your resume to each specific job description. This takes 15 minutes per application but dramatically improves your ATS score.

Copy the job description into a document. Highlight every skill, tool, and responsibility mentioned more than once. Check your resume against each one. Add the ones you have but didn't mention. Note the ones you're missing — those are your gaps.
