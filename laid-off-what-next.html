For people in transition

Just got laid off.
What to do next.

The first 48 hours after a layoff feel like a combination of relief, shock, and dread. This guide skips the platitudes. Here's what to actually do — in order — to move from disruption to direction.

7 minute read — written for people in this exact situation right now.

First — give yourself
24 hours.

Not to job search. Not to update your LinkedIn. Just 24 hours to process what happened before making any significant decisions.

Layoffs — especially unexpected ones — trigger real stress responses. Decisions made in that state are often not the best ones. The impulse to immediately fire off applications, announce on LinkedIn, or pivot to a completely new career is understandable but rarely serves you.

In the first 24 hours: tell the people close to you. Take a walk. Sleep. That's it. Everything else can wait one day.

What actually needs to happen in the first week

Understand your severance and benefits situation · File for unemployment if eligible · Review your finances — how long can you run? · Tell people in your network what happened · Update LinkedIn status to "Open to Work" (optional but high-value) · Do NOT send mass applications or make major decisions yet

The first month.
What matters.

01

Get clear on what you actually want next

A layoff is a forced pause. Used well, it's a chance to be deliberate about what comes next — rather than just finding the fastest replacement for what you just left. Take a week to ask: was that the right direction? Is there a role I've been wanting to move toward? What would I regret not trying?

02

Understand your real financial runway

Calculate specifically how many months you can run on savings plus severance plus any unemployment benefits. This number determines how much time pressure you're actually under. Most people overestimate the urgency and make worse decisions as a result. Know your number.

03

Get a real picture of your market value

What roles are you genuinely competitive for right now? Not what you'd like to be competitive for — what can you get an interview for based on your current skills and experience? This requires honest assessment. Leapr's gap analysis helps here — it shows you specifically where you stand for your target role.

04

Reach out to your network — personally, not mass

Not a LinkedIn post saying "I'm open to opportunities". Personal messages to 10–15 specific people you respect: a former manager, a former colleague, someone in a role you're interested in. Tell them what happened, what you're looking for, and ask if they know of anything or anyone relevant. This is how most jobs are found.

If you're thinking about
changing direction.

Layoffs are often a catalyst for career change — and that's not a bad thing. But the window is finite and the stakes are real, so the pivot needs to be strategic, not just emotional.

The questions to answer before pivoting

What's the real distance between my current skills and my target role? How long will it realistically take to close the critical gaps? How long can I afford to spend before I need income? Is there an adjacent move that uses more of what I already have and gets me there faster?

The most common mistake in post-layoff pivots is choosing a target role based on what sounds exciting — without understanding what the move actually requires. A pivot from marketing manager to product manager sounds straightforward. The actual gap — user research, product discovery, roadmap experience — can take 6–12 months to close credibly.
