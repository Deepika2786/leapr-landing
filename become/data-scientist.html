The role

What does a Data Scientist
actually do?

A Data Scientist extracts insight from data to drive decisions. You build models, run experiments, analyse patterns, and communicate findings to non-technical stakeholders. The job sits at the intersection of statistics, programming, and business judgment.

The day-to-day varies enormously by company. At a startup you might do everything from ETL to ML deployment. At a large company you might own one very specific slice — like churn prediction or pricing models. Either way, the core is the same: use data to answer questions that matter.

You will do a lot of

Analysis & modelling

Exploring datasets, building predictive models, validating results, iterating.

You will do a lot of

Communication

Translating complex findings into clear business recommendations non-technical people can act on.

You will do less of

Pure research

Most industry roles are applied — solving real business problems, not publishing papers.

You will do less of

Building data pipelines

That's more data engineering. Some overlap exists but DS focuses on insight, not infrastructure.

Skills

What skills does a
Data Scientist need?

The core skillset has three layers — statistics, programming, and domain knowledge. Most career changers already have domain knowledge. The technical skills are learnable.

Technical skills

These are the non-negotiables. Build them systematically.

Python (pandas, numpy, sklearn) SQL — advanced Statistics & probability Machine learning fundamentals Data visualisation A/B testing & experimentation Feature engineering Model evaluation

Business skills

Often underrated — these separate good data scientists from great ones.

Problem framing Stakeholder communication Business acumen Critical thinking Intellectual curiosity

The path

Your step-by-step
roadmap.

1
Month 1–2

Master Python and SQL

Python is non-negotiable. Start with pandas and numpy for data manipulation. Advanced SQL is equally important — most hiring processes include a SQL test. Kaggle and Mode Analytics are good starting points.

2
Month 2–4

Learn statistics and ML fundamentals

Take a statistics course (StatQuest on YouTube is excellent). Then work through the fundamentals of machine learning — regression, classification, clustering, model evaluation. Scikit-learn is the standard library.

3
Month 3–6

Build real projects

Kaggle competitions are a start but not enough. Find a real dataset in your industry and solve a real problem. Document your approach, your decisions, and your results. One strong project beats ten toy datasets.

4
Month 5–9

Build your portfolio and GitHub

Every project should be on GitHub with a clear README explaining the problem, your approach, and what you found. Hiring managers look at GitHub. Make it tell a story about how you think.

5
Month 8–18

Target the right first role

Start with Data Analyst roles if you need a stepping stone — many analysts transition to DS within 12–18 months. Target companies in your domain. Your industry knowledge is a genuine advantage over pure technical candidates.
