The role

What does a AI Governance Specialist
actually do?

An AI Governance Specialist ensures that an organisation's use of AI is responsible, compliant, and aligned with ethical principles. You assess AI systems for risk and bias, develop governance frameworks and policies, ensure regulatory compliance, and advise leadership on responsible AI deployment.

This role sits at the intersection of technology, policy, law, and ethics. As AI becomes embedded in consequential decisions — hiring, lending, healthcare, criminal justice — the need for people who can critically evaluate these systems and hold them accountable is urgent and growing.

You will do a lot of

AI risk assessment

Evaluating AI systems for bias, fairness, transparency, and safety risks before and after deployment.

You will do a lot of

Policy and framework development

Creating governance structures, acceptable use policies, and internal standards that guide how the organisation develops and deploys AI.

You will do less of

Building AI systems

That's AI and ML Engineering. Governance specialists evaluate and oversee systems, not build them.

You will do less of

Pure compliance checking

Good AI governance is proactive — it shapes how AI is developed, not just whether it passes an audit.

The path

Your step-by-step
roadmap.

This is the typical path for someone transitioning into ai governance specialist. Your actual timeline depends on your starting point and how much time you invest.

1
Month 1–2

Build AI literacy

You need to understand how AI systems work well enough to identify risks and evaluate claims. Take a technical AI course — fast.ai or Coursera's AI for Everyone. Read Anthropic and DeepMind's safety research. You don't need to build models but you need to understand what they do.

2
Month 2–4

Study AI governance frameworks

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework is the US standard — read it thoroughly. The EU AI Act is the most significant regulation globally — understand its risk categories. Read widely on AI ethics: Weapons of Math Destruction, The Alignment Problem, and Atlas of AI are essential.

3
Month 3–6

Develop policy and risk skills

If your background is in law, compliance, or risk — this is where your existing skills directly apply. Learn to write AI governance policies, acceptable use frameworks, and risk assessment rubrics. Study how companies like Google, Microsoft, and Anthropic have structured their AI ethics functions.

4
Month 4–8

Get involved in the field

The AI governance community is active and accessible. Join the Partnership on AI, the AI Now Institute events, and government consultations on AI regulation. Write publicly about AI governance topics in your domain. This builds credibility and network simultaneously.

5
Month 6–14

Target governance roles

AI Ethics Officer, Responsible AI Lead, AI Policy Manager, and AI Risk Analyst are the common titles. Financial services, healthcare, government, and large tech companies are the biggest employers. Your previous career domain is your differentiator — a healthcare AI governance specialist who understands clinical workflows is immediately more valuable in that sector.

AI Safety (as used in the research community) typically refers to the technical problem of ensuring advanced AI systems remain aligned with human values — especially as they become more capable. AI Governance typically refers to the organisational and regulatory structures for ensuring current AI systems are used responsibly. Both matter; AI Governance is the more immediately actionable career path.
