The role

What does a Product Designer
actually do?

A Product Designer owns the end-to-end design of digital products — from user research and wireframing through to high-fidelity visual design and developer handoff. Unlike UX Designers who focus more on research and flows, Product Designers typically do both the UX and UI work.

Product Design has become the dominant title for designers in tech, replacing the older UX/UI split. You're expected to be a complete designer — able to define the problem through research and deliver polished, production-ready visual designs. The role rewards both analytical thinking and strong visual craft.

You will do a lot of

End-to-end design

From user research and problem definition through wireframing, prototyping, visual design, and handoff to engineering.

You will do a lot of

Cross-functional collaboration

Working closely with product managers to define requirements and with engineers to ensure designs are implemented correctly.

You will do less of

Writing code

Product Designers don't typically write production code, though knowing HTML/CSS helps.

You will do less of

Marketing design

Product Design focuses on the product experience. Brand and marketing design are separate disciplines.

The path

Your step-by-step
roadmap.

This is the typical path for someone transitioning into product designer. Your actual timeline depends on your starting point and how much time you invest.

1
Month 1–3

Master Figma and visual design fundamentals

Figma is non-negotiable. Beyond the tool — study visual design principles: grid systems, typography, colour theory, spacing, and hierarchy. Study apps you admire and dissect why they work visually. Read 'The Design of Everyday Things' for UX fundamentals.

2
Month 2–5

Build UX research skills

Product Designers need research skills, not just visual skills. Learn user interview techniques, usability testing, and how to translate research into design decisions. Run real interviews with real users — even on apps you're redesigning as a portfolio project.

3
Month 3–7

Build 3 strong case studies

Each should show your full process: research findings, wireframes, design iterations, final high-fidelity designs, and what you'd measure. Include real decisions and trade-offs — not just the final polished output. Employers hire for process, not just aesthetics.

4
Month 5–9

Learn design systems

Every mature product company uses a design system. Learn how to build and maintain components in Figma. Contribute to or study open design systems (Material Design, Apple HIG, Atlassian Design System). This is a differentiating skill for mid and senior roles.

5
Month 8–14

Target product design roles

Junior Product Designer, Product Designer, and UI/UX Designer are common titles. Startup roles give you broader ownership; larger companies give you more specialisation and mentorship. Your previous domain expertise is valuable — a Product Designer who understands finance, healthcare, or education is immediately more useful in those verticals.
