The role

What does a Data Analyst
actually do?

A Data Analyst answers business questions using data. You pull data from databases, clean and analyse it, identify patterns and insights, and communicate findings to decision-makers. The goal is always the same: help the organisation make better decisions faster.

Data Analyst is one of the most accessible roles in tech because it rewards analytical thinking over deep technical skill, and because every industry needs it. Finance, healthcare, retail, marketing, operations — the role exists everywhere, and your previous career domain makes you immediately more valuable in it.

You will do a lot of

Data extraction and analysis

Writing SQL queries, pulling data from multiple sources, cleaning messy data, and building the datasets that analysis runs on.

You will do a lot of

Insight communication

Building dashboards, writing reports, and presenting findings to stakeholders — translating data into decisions.

You will do less of

Building ML models

That's Data Science. Analysts describe what happened; scientists predict what will happen.

You will do less of

Building data pipelines

That's Data Engineering. Analysts use the data infrastructure; engineers build it.

The path

Your step-by-step
roadmap.

This is the typical path for someone transitioning into data analyst. Your actual timeline depends on your starting point and how much time you invest.

1
Month 1–2

Master SQL

SQL is the single most important skill for data analysts. Start with SELECT, WHERE, GROUP BY, JOIN. Then move to window functions, CTEs, and subqueries. Mode Analytics, SQLZoo, and StrataScratch all have excellent free practice environments. You should be comfortable writing complex queries within 6–8 weeks.

2
Month 2–4

Learn a visualisation tool

Tableau and Power BI are the two dominant tools — learn whichever is more common in your target industry (Tableau is stronger in larger companies and consulting; Power BI is dominant in Microsoft-heavy enterprises). Build dashboards with real data. Kaggle has excellent public datasets to practise with.

3
Month 3–6

Build your portfolio

Find a real dataset in your current industry and answer a real business question with it. Document your approach, your SQL, your visualisation, and your insight. This is your case study. Do 2–3 of these across different business problems.

4
Month 4–7

Add Python or R basics

Not essential for entry-level roles, but Python with pandas and matplotlib significantly expands what you can analyse and how fast. Focus on data manipulation and visualisation — you don't need ML libraries yet.

5
Month 6–10

Target analyst roles

Data Analyst, Business Analyst, and Marketing Analyst are the most common entry titles. Your previous career domain is your strongest differentiator — a Data Analyst who deeply understands healthcare, finance, logistics, or retail is immediately more valuable in that vertical than a generalist.

FAQ

Common questions.

It's one of the best. SQL is learnable in a few months, every industry needs analysts, and your previous domain expertise is immediately valuable. Healthcare analysts, retail analysts, operations analysts — these roles value industry knowledge as much as technical skill. The barrier to entry is lower than most tech roles.
Data Analysts describe and explain past data. Data Scientists predict future outcomes and build models. Analyst is the more accessible starting point — lower technical requirements, faster to hire-ready, and a clear path to Data Scientist if you choose. Many Data Scientists started as analysts.
SQL and a visualisation tool (Tableau or Power BI) will get you into most entry-level roles. Python adds power and speed but isn't universally required. Learn SQL first, then add Python once you have a job. Many excellent senior analysts use only SQL and Tableau.
Public datasets are your friend. Kaggle, government data portals (data.gov, NHS data), and company reports all have real data you can analyse. Pick topics you know — if you're a nurse, analyse NHS waiting time data. Domain knowledge makes your analysis more insightful than someone without it.
Finance, tech, consulting, and healthcare typically pay the highest. However, your domain expertise in almost any industry can command a premium if combined with strong analytical skills. A supply chain analyst who deeply understands logistics is worth significantly more than a generalist in that sector.
