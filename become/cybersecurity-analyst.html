The role

What does a Cybersecurity Analyst
actually do?

A Cybersecurity Analyst protects an organisation's systems, networks, and data from cyber threats. You monitor for suspicious activity, investigate incidents, assess vulnerabilities, and implement controls to reduce risk.

The field is broad — you might specialise in threat intelligence, incident response, vulnerability management, SOC analysis, or GRC (governance, risk, and compliance). The common thread is a defender's mindset: thinking like an attacker to build better defences.

You will do a lot of

Monitoring and detection

Reviewing alerts, investigating anomalies, and determining whether events are real threats or false positives.

You will do a lot of

Vulnerability assessment

Scanning systems for weaknesses, prioritising remediation, and working with teams to fix issues.

You will do less of

Offensive hacking

That's penetration testing — a related but separate specialisation. Analysts are primarily defenders.

You will do less of

Software development

Some scripting helps but cybersecurity analysts are not primarily developers.

The path

Your step-by-step
roadmap.

This is the typical path for someone transitioning into cybersecurity analyst. Your actual timeline depends on your starting point and how much time you invest.

1
Month 1–3

Build the foundations

CompTIA Security+ is the industry-standard entry-level certification — get it. Study networking fundamentals, operating systems, and security concepts. TryHackMe and Hack The Box have free hands-on labs. The foundation matters more than rushing into specialisation.

2
Month 2–5

Get hands-on with tools

Set up a home lab. Practice with Splunk (free tier available), learn to use Nessus or OpenVAS for vulnerability scanning, and practise log analysis. Employers want people who've actually used the tools, not just read about them.

3
Month 4–8

Choose a specialisation

SOC Analyst, GRC Analyst, Vulnerability Management, and Cloud Security are the most accessible entry points. GRC is especially good for career changers with legal, compliance, or audit backgrounds. SOC is good for people who like fast-paced investigation.

4
Month 6–10

Pursue certifications

Beyond Security+, consider CompTIA CySA+, GIAC GSEC, or for GRC, CISM or CRISC. Certifications are currency in cybersecurity — they signal commitment and baseline competence to employers.

5
Month 10–18

Target your first role

SOC Analyst Level 1 and IT Security Analyst are the standard entry points. Managed Security Service Providers (MSSPs) are good first employers — high volume of incidents builds skills quickly. Government and defence also have strong entry pathways.
