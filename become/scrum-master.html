The role

What does a Scrum Master
actually do?

A Scrum Master facilitates the Scrum framework within software development teams. You run ceremonies (sprint planning, daily standups, retrospectives, sprint reviews), remove impediments, coach the team on agile practices, and protect the team from external disruptions so they can focus on delivery.

The Scrum Master is a servant leader — your job is to make everyone else's job easier. You're not a project manager (you don't own the plan), not a product manager (you don't own the roadmap), and not a developer (you don't write code). You facilitate and coach.

You will do a lot of

Facilitating Scrum ceremonies

Running effective sprint planning, standups, retrospectives, and reviews — making them useful rather than ceremonial.

You will do a lot of

Removing impediments

Identifying blockers that slow the team and proactively resolving them — whether organisational, technical, or interpersonal.

You will do less of

Making product decisions

That's the Product Owner's role. Scrum Masters don't prioritise the backlog.

You will do less of

Managing people

Scrum Masters have no line management authority. You lead through influence and coaching, not hierarchy.

The path

Your step-by-step
roadmap.

This is the typical path for someone transitioning into scrum master. Your actual timeline depends on your starting point and how much time you invest.

1
Month 1–2

Get CSM certified

Certified ScrumMaster (CSM) from Scrum Alliance is the standard entry-level credential. It requires a 2-day course and a simple exam. PSM I from Scrum.org is also widely respected and cheaper. Get one, study the Scrum Guide thoroughly.

2
Month 1–3

Learn facilitation techniques

The Scrum Master role is 70% facilitation. Learn liberating structures, retrospective formats (Start/Stop/Continue, 4Ls, Sailboat), and how to run meetings that actually generate insight. Try facilitating meetings in your current role.

3
Month 2–5

Understand agile at scale

Most companies use modified or scaled agile. Learn SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework), Kanban, and how Scrum integrates with non-Scrum teams. SAFe certification is valuable at enterprise companies.

4
Month 3–7

Get practical experience

This is the catch — most Scrum Master roles want experience. Bridge this by: becoming an unofficial Scrum Master on your current team, volunteering at a non-profit, or taking an entry-level agile coordinator role. The CSM gets you past the filter; experience gets you the job.

5
Month 6–10

Target Scrum Master roles

Junior Scrum Master, Agile Coach (entry), and Agile Delivery Manager are common titles. Companies undergoing digital transformation are the biggest hirers. Your previous career domain is an asset — a Scrum Master who understands financial services, healthcare, or manufacturing is more valuable than a generalist.
