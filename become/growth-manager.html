The role

What does a Growth Manager
actually do?

A Growth Manager drives measurable improvements in user acquisition, activation, retention, and revenue through systematic experimentation. You run A/B tests, analyse funnels, identify growth levers, and work across product, marketing, and engineering to execute growth initiatives.

Growth is one of the most data-intensive roles in business. The best Growth Managers combine analytical rigour with creative thinking — they know when a metric is telling the truth and when it's misleading, and they're equally comfortable writing a hypothesis and interrogating a SQL query.

You will do a lot of

Experimentation and A/B testing

Designing, running, and analysing experiments to improve conversion, activation, and retention metrics.

You will do a lot of

Funnel analysis

Identifying where users drop off in the acquisition or activation journey and designing interventions to fix it.

You will do less of

Brand marketing

Growth is performance-focused and measurable. Brand building is a different discipline with a different timeframe.

You will do less of

Long-term product strategy

Growth Managers optimise existing systems. Product strategy is owned by PMs.

The path

Your step-by-step
roadmap.

This is the typical path for someone transitioning into growth manager. Your actual timeline depends on your starting point and how much time you invest.

1
Month 1–2

Learn growth fundamentals

Read 'Hacking Growth' by Sean Ellis and 'Lean Analytics' by Alistair Croll. Understand the AARRR framework (Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue). Study how the best growth teams at companies like Dropbox, Slack, and Airbnb worked.

2
Month 2–4

Build your analytics skills

SQL is essential — learn it well enough to pull your own data. Then pick one product analytics tool (Amplitude or Mixpanel) and learn it deeply. Understand cohort analysis, retention curves, and funnel analysis. These are the core tools of growth.

3
Month 3–6

Run actual experiments

The fastest way to learn growth is to run experiments. If you're currently in a marketing or product role, propose A/B tests. If not, run experiments on a side project — even a simple landing page has enough surface area to test headlines, CTAs, and onboarding flows.

4
Month 4–8

Build a growth portfolio

Document experiments you've designed and run — hypothesis, methodology, results, and what you learned. Even failed experiments demonstrate the right thinking. Growth hiring managers care about your process more than your wins.

5
Month 8–14

Target growth roles

Growth Analyst, Growth Associate, and Marketing Analyst are common entry points. Start-ups and growth-stage companies (Series A–C) are the best learning environments. Your previous career domain is an advantage — a Growth Manager who understands healthcare, finance, or education is immediately more valuable in those verticals.
