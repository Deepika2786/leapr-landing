What skills-based hiring
actually means.

Skills-based hiring means companies evaluate what you can do — not where you studied, what your job title was, or how many years you've been in the industry. Instead of "5 years experience required", you see "must be able to build a data pipeline" or "must demonstrate product discovery skills".

This shift is being driven by two things. First, AI and automation are changing roles faster than credentials can track. A degree from 4 years ago doesn't tell an employer whether you can use the tools that exist today. Second, companies have started realising they were missing excellent candidates by filtering on pedigree rather than ability.

What companies are
actually testing.

Skills-based hiring shows up in different ways depending on the company and role. Understanding which format you'll face helps you prepare specifically.

01

Technical assessments

A coding challenge, a SQL test, a case study, a design critique. These are increasingly used before interviews to filter candidates on actual ability. They're often timed and done at home. The best preparation is doing the real work — not studying for the test specifically.

02

Portfolio review

Especially for design, product, data, and engineering roles. Your portfolio is evaluated before or during the interview. What matters is not polish — it's evidence of thinking. The decisions you made and why matter more than the outcome looking beautiful.

03

Skills-based interview questions

"Walk me through how you'd prioritise this product backlog." "Given this dataset, what story does it tell?" "How would you approach debugging this system?" These are behavioural and technical questions that require demonstrated ability to answer — not just experience to describe.

04

Work samples

Some companies ask for a work sample as part of the application — a short analysis, a design mock, a written strategy. These are increasingly common in data, product, and creative roles.
