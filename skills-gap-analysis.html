What a skills gap analysis
actually is.

A skills gap analysis is a structured comparison between the skills your target role requires and the skills you currently have. The difference — the gap — is what you need to address before you can be competitive for that role.

Sounds simple. Most people skip it entirely. They apply based on a rough sense that they're qualified, then wonder why they're not getting calls. The gap is usually specific, not general. It's not "I don't have enough experience" — it's "this role requires dbt and I've never used it" or "they want product discovery experience and my resume shows zero user research".

Specific gaps are solvable. General feelings of inadequacy are not. That's why doing this properly matters.

What a good gap analysis tells you

Exactly which skills your target role requires in 2026 · Which of those you already have · Which you're missing · Which gaps are critical (blocking) vs minor (easy to address) · What to focus on first to become competitive fastest

How to do it
manually.

If you want to do this yourself before using a tool — here's the honest process.

01

Collect 5 job descriptions for your target role

Not 1 — 5. From different companies, different sizes, different industries. One JD might be outdated or written by someone who doesn't know what they actually need. Five gives you a pattern.

02

Extract every skill mentioned more than twice

Go through all 5 JDs. Every time a skill, tool, or capability is mentioned — note it. Skills mentioned across multiple JDs are the ones that actually matter. Skills mentioned once might be a nice-to-have or an oversight.

03

Map your current skills honestly against the list

For each required skill: do you have it? Can you demonstrate it? Not "I know roughly what this is" — can you do it, show it, talk about it in an interview with specific examples? Be honest. Overestimating your skills leads to underprepared interviews.

04

Classify each gap as critical, moderate, or minor

Critical: appears in every JD, likely tested in interview. Moderate: appears in most JDs, expected but maybe not tested. Minor: appears occasionally, nice-to-have. Focus on critical gaps first.

05

Build a specific closing plan for critical gaps

For each critical gap — what's the fastest legitimate way to close it? A specific course? A project? A certification? Set a timeline. "I will have basic dbt proficiency in 4 weeks by completing X and building Y project."

The common mistakes
people make.

Analysing the wrong role

If your target is "Product Manager" but you're analysing "Senior Product Manager" JDs — your gap analysis is wrong. Be specific about the level, industry, and company stage you're targeting. A startup PM role and an enterprise PM role have different requirements.

Using outdated job descriptions

A JD posted 18 months ago reflects what companies needed 18 months ago. In fast-moving fields — AI, data, cloud — that's a long time. Use current JDs. Posted in the last 3 months is the right filter.

Confusing familiarity with competence

"I know what Kubernetes is" is not the same as "I can deploy and manage a Kubernetes cluster". Be honest about the difference between awareness, familiarity, and genuine competence. Hiring managers will test the difference.

Trying to close all gaps at once

You don't need to close every gap before applying. You need to close the critical ones and have a credible plan for the moderate ones. Paralysis — waiting until you're perfect — is the most common career transition mistake.
