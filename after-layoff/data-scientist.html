First 7 days

What to do
this week.

The data science job market has stratified. Applied ML, LLM-adjacent work, and data science with strong engineering skills are in demand. Pure analytics and notebook-only data science is more competitive. Knowing which category describes you shapes everything else.

Day 1–2

File unemployment and get your financial runway clear

Data science searches take slightly longer than engineering searches on average. Knowing you have 4–6 months of runway removes the panic that leads to accepting roles below your market value.

Day 2–3

Audit your GitHub and Kaggle profiles immediately

Data science hiring managers look at GitHub and Kaggle before they read your resume. Is your best project public? Is your Kaggle profile active? If not, prioritise this over resume polish in week one.

Day 3–5

Identify whether you're applied ML or analytics DS

Applied ML (model building, deployment, ML pipelines) and analytics data science (SQL, dashboards, A/B tests) have different job markets right now. Applied ML is stronger. Honestly assess where your skills sit and target accordingly — applying to the wrong category wastes weeks.

Day 5–7

Update LinkedIn with your specific models and frameworks

"Machine learning experience" is invisible. "Built gradient boosting churn model reducing customer attrition by 18% — XGBoost, scikit-learn, deployed via FastAPI" is findable and memorable. Be specific about what you built and what it achieved.

Your assets

What you have that
the market wants.

Highest demand

Deployed ML models and production experience

Data scientists who've taken models from notebook to production — via FastAPI, Flask, or cloud endpoints — are significantly more hireable than those whose work lives in Jupyter notebooks. If you have production deployment experience, lead with it.

High demand

LLM and AI product experience

Data scientists who've worked with LLM APIs, RAG pipelines, or AI feature development are in strong demand from AI-first companies. Even side project experience with OpenAI or Anthropic APIs is worth surfacing prominently.

Strong signal

Domain expertise — finance, health, e-commerce

A data scientist with deep domain knowledge in your previous industry is more valuable to companies in that space than a generalist. Target companies in your sector explicitly — your domain knowledge is a genuine competitive advantage.

Differentiator

Strong SQL and data engineering skills

Data scientists who can also write production-quality SQL, build simple pipelines, and work without a dedicated data engineer behind them are significantly more hireable at smaller companies. If this describes you, position it explicitly.

Strong demand

AI-first product companies

Companies building AI-powered products need data scientists who understand both the modelling and the product side. If you have any LLM or AI product experience, these companies should be your primary target.

Stable demand

Financial services and insurance

Risk modelling, fraud detection, and customer analytics at financial companies continues to hire steadily. These roles are often more stable than pure tech companies and value your domain expertise if you have finance background.

Emerging opportunity

AI tools and infrastructure companies

Companies building tools for data scientists and ML engineers — MLflow, Weights & Biases, Hugging Face, and dozens of startups — hire data scientists to work on their own products and be internal champions. These are interesting and well-compensated roles.

Consider broadening

ML Engineer titles

If your experience includes model deployment and production ML work, applying to ML Engineer roles significantly expands your market. The title often pays more and has stronger demand than data scientist roles right now.

90-day plan

Your roadmap to
next offer.

1
Week 1–2

Activate your network and clean up your public presence

GitHub and Kaggle visible and active. LinkedIn updated with specific models and business impact. 15 personalised messages to former colleagues and data community contacts. Leapr analysis done.

2
Week 2–5

Apply to companies where your domain expertise creates advantage

Target companies in your previous industry first — your domain knowledge is your differentiator. Then expand to adjacent sectors. 15–20 tailored applications per week, each with resume language mirroring the specific tools and frameworks in the job description.

3
Week 3–6

Prepare your model stories for technical interviews

Every data science interview asks you to walk through a model you built. Prepare 2–3 specific stories: the business problem, your data, the approach you chose and why, how you evaluated it, the result, and what you'd do differently. Numbers — lift, precision, revenue impact — are what make stories memorable.

4
Week 4–8

Build parallel processes and create urgency

Keep multiple active processes running. Never evaluate one offer in isolation. Use competing timelines as leverage even when you only have soft interest from other companies — "I have another process moving quickly" is true leverage if your pipeline is active.

5
Week 6–14

Negotiate your full package

Data science roles at AI companies often include equity that can be more valuable than the base salary differential. Understand the equity structure, vesting schedule, and strike price before comparing offers. Counter on total comp, not just base.

What not to do

Mistakes that
cost you weeks.

Applying broadly without distinguishing analytics DS from applied ML roles

These are different markets with different technical bars. Applying to both without tailoring your positioning creates a generic profile that stands out in neither. Identify your stronger side and lead with it.

Leading with your academic credentials instead of your business impact

"PhD in Statistics" buried under impact statements is fine. "PhD in Statistics" as your primary differentiator — when hiring managers want to know what models you shipped and what they achieved — is the wrong emphasis for most industry roles.

Not practising SQL interview questions because you use it every day

Interview SQL is different — it's time-pressured and tests specific patterns. Practice on StrataScratch and DataLemur regardless of your actual SQL fluency. The format matters as much as the knowledge.

Waiting to update Kaggle and GitHub until your resume is perfect

Your public technical presence is evaluated before your resume. Prioritise it in week one, not after everything else is polished.
