First 7 days

What to do
this week.

ML engineering is a relationship and visibility-driven market. The best roles come through warm introductions and recruiter outreach — not cold applications. Your first week should prioritise being findable and activating your network.

Day 1–2

File unemployment and calculate your runway

ML engineering searches take 6–14 weeks on average because the interview process is longer — typically 4–6 rounds including a technical screen, ML system design, and often a take-home. Knowing your runway removes pressure from a process that rewards patience.

Day 2–3

Update LinkedIn with specific models and frameworks

"Machine learning experience" is invisible. "Built production recommendation system serving 2M users daily — PyTorch, Kafka, SageMaker endpoints, <50ms p99 latency" is findable and memorable. Lead with what you built and the scale it operated at.

Day 3–5

Make your best work visible on GitHub

ML hiring managers look at GitHub to evaluate whether you write production-quality code or notebook code. Add a README to your strongest project explaining the problem, your approach, evaluation results, and deployment architecture. Even a well-documented personal project demonstrates production thinking.

Day 5–7

Reach out to ML recruiters and your network directly

Specialised ML recruiters (Harnham, Insight Global's data division, and boutique AI search firms) actively place ML engineers and have roles that aren't publicly listed. Message 10–15 former colleagues and ML community contacts — conferences, Slack communities, open source contributors you've interacted with.

Your assets

What you have that
the market wants.

Highest demand

Production model deployment experience

ML engineers who've served models in production — with real latency constraints, monitoring, and retraining pipelines — are significantly more valuable than those who only train models in notebooks. Quantify: requests per second, latency SLOs, uptime requirements.

High demand

LLM fine-tuning and RAG pipeline experience

Experience with LLM fine-tuning (LoRA, PEFT), RAG architecture, or building LLM evaluation frameworks is in extremely strong demand from AI-first companies. Even significant personal project experience here is worth surfacing prominently.

Strong signal

MLOps and model monitoring

Engineers who've built training pipelines, managed model registries (MLflow, W&B), and set up drift detection are rare. If you have MLOps depth, it belongs in your headline — many job descriptions specifically search for it.

Differentiator

Strong software engineering fundamentals

ML engineers who write clean, tested, documented code are meaningfully rarer than those who produce good models but messy code. Evidence of software engineering quality — PR history, open source contributions, well-structured repositories — differentiates you in interviews.

Strongest demand

AI-first product companies

Companies building AI-powered products — Anthropic, OpenAI, Cohere, Mistral, and hundreds of AI application companies — are actively hiring ML engineers. These roles come with the most interesting technical problems and often significant equity.

Stable demand

Large tech companies with ML infra teams

Meta, Google, Microsoft, and Apple hire ML engineers for recommendation, search, ads, and foundation model infrastructure. These roles are competitive but pay the highest total comp in the market.

Growing fast

Companies helping enterprises deploy AI — Databricks, Scale AI, Weights & Biases, Hugging Face — hire ML engineers to work on their own products and support customers. Excellent technical environments with strong growth.

Consider targeting

AI Engineer titles

AI Engineer roles (LLM application development) are more numerous than pure ML Engineer roles right now. If your experience includes LLM integration and prompt engineering alongside classical ML, expanding to AI Engineer titles significantly increases your pool.

90-day plan

Your roadmap to
next offer.

1
Week 1–2

Get visible and activate your ML community network

LinkedIn updated with specific models and scale. GitHub public with production-quality projects. ML recruiters contacted. 10 personalised messages to ML community contacts. Leapr analysis done.

2
Week 2–5

Apply to AI-first companies specifically

Target companies where ML is the product — not a feature. Your experience is most valued where the technical bar is highest and the impact of good ML work is most visible. 10–15 targeted applications per week, each tailored to the specific model types and frameworks in the job description.

3
Week 3–7

Prepare for ML system design interviews intensively

ML interviews have a distinct format: ML system design (design a recommendation system for X), ML theory (explain the bias-variance tradeoff in the context of Y), coding (implement this ML algorithm from scratch), and production case studies. Prepare specific answers for each category. ML system design is where most candidates underperform relative to their actual experience.

4
Week 5–10

Build parallel processes — ML interviews take time

ML interview processes run 4–6 weeks from first contact to offer. Start multiple processes simultaneously so they converge on the same timeframe. When one reaches offer stage, you'll have real competing options rather than artificial urgency.

5
Week 8–14

Negotiate total compensation — equity matters most at AI companies

At AI startups, equity can be worth multiples of your base salary over a 4-year vest. Understand the company's valuation, the equity pool, your percentage, and the vesting schedule before comparing offers. Base salary is only one part of the picture.

What not to do

Mistakes that
cost you weeks.

Applying to research roles when your experience is applied ML

Research ML roles at labs require publication records and typically PhD-level credentials. Applied ML engineering roles at product companies are a very different market. Applying to roles that don't match your background wastes applications at companies you can't reapply to for 6+ months.

Describing your models without production context

"Trained a churn prediction model" is forgettable. "Trained a churn prediction model that reduced voluntary churn by 14% — gradient boosting, 6-month feature window, deployed via SageMaker endpoint serving 40k predictions daily" is memorable and interview-generating.

Skipping ML system design preparation

Most ML engineers over-prepare for ML theory and under-prepare for ML system design. The system design round — design a recommendation system, design a fraud detection system — is where the most offers are lost. Practice this specifically with a clock running.

Only targeting large tech companies

FAANG ML processes take 8–16 weeks. AI startups can move in 3–4 weeks. Run both tracks simultaneously rather than waiting for large company processes to conclude before starting others.

FAQ

Questions you're
probably asking.

Target AI-first companies first — they have the strongest demand for ML engineers, the most interesting problems, and the highest compensation. Broaden to companies with significant applied ML functions (recommendation, fraud, personalisation) in week 3–4 if your AI-first pipeline isn't building fast enough. Don't start broad — your skills are most valued where ML is the core product.
Classical ML is still the backbone of most production ML at scale — recommendation systems, fraud, forecasting, ranking. Don't feel you need to become an LLM specialist to be competitive. Do build enough LLM literacy to speak credibly about when LLMs are and aren't the right tool. A weekend project using the OpenAI API gives you enough experience to address LLM questions honestly in interviews.
ML interview processes are genuinely longer than most engineering interviews — 4–6 weeks from first contact to offer is standard, not a sign something is wrong. Companies run ML system design, multiple technical rounds, and sometimes a paid project. The solution is to have 5–6 active processes running simultaneously so multiple offers converge on the same timeframe rather than evaluating each sequentially.
A lateral move is fine if the company, team, and technical problem are right. A title step down at a significantly stronger company or more interesting technical environment is often worth it — title is reversible, the experience you accumulate isn't. What to avoid: taking a step down in technical scope (moving from production ML to analytics or internal tooling) — that takes longer to recover from than a title adjustment.
