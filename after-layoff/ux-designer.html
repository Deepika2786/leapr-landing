First 7 days

What to do
this week.

UX design is the most portfolio-dependent hiring market in tech. Your portfolio quality determines your callback rate more than your years of experience, your LinkedIn activity, or your network. The first week is about auditing your portfolio honestly before sending a single application.

Day 1–2

File unemployment and audit your portfolio ruthlessly

Before anything else, evaluate your portfolio with fresh eyes — or better, ask a designer friend to review it. Does each case study show the full process (research → decisions → design → testing → outcome) or just final screens? Portfolios showing only final screens get skipped. Fix this before sending applications.

Day 2–3

Add impact metrics to your case studies immediately

"Redesigned the onboarding flow" is generic. "Redesigned onboarding flow — reduced time to first value by 40%, improved D7 retention by 12%" is specific and memorable. Go back through your employment history and find the numbers behind your work. Even rough estimates with context are better than no metrics.

Day 3–5

Update your LinkedIn and share your portfolio link prominently

Your portfolio URL should be the first thing on your LinkedIn profile — above your About section. Recruiters spend 30 seconds on a designer's profile before deciding whether to click through. Make clicking your portfolio the easiest action available to them.

Day 5–7

Get portfolio feedback before mass applying

Share your portfolio in ADPList, Designer Hangout Slack, or with 2–3 working designers in your network. Ask specifically: "Does this case study make clear why I made the design decisions I made?" One piece of specific feedback before you apply is worth more than 20 applications with a weak portfolio.

Your assets

What you have that
the market wants.

Primary credential

Shipped product in your portfolio

Case studies showing shipped features — with before/after metrics — are significantly more compelling than redesign concepts or speculative projects. If you have shipped work, make it the first case study in your portfolio. If you don't, that's the most important gap to address.

Strong differentiator

Domain expertise from your previous company

A UX designer who spent 3 years in fintech understands financial workflows at a depth that general designers don't. Lead with your domain knowledge when applying to companies in your sector — it's your competitive advantage over designers with similar tool skills but different backgrounds.

Valued at all levels

User research depth — not just usability testing

Designers who can conduct substantive user interviews, synthesise findings, and demonstrate how research shaped their design decisions are meaningfully more hireable than those who only run usability tests on finished designs. Surface your research depth explicitly in your portfolio narrative.

Growing demand

AI product design experience

Designers who've worked on AI-powered features — particularly around uncertainty communication, trust design, and fallback experiences — are in growing demand. Even thinking publicly about AI UX on LinkedIn or in a case study addendum signals relevant awareness.

Most accessible

Product companies with dedicated design teams

SaaS companies, consumer apps, and fintech companies with established design functions are the most reliable UX hiring market. They evaluate on portfolio quality and move faster than enterprises. Target companies with at least 5 designers — they're mature enough to value the role properly.

Active hiring

AI-first product companies

AI product companies need designers who can think about novel interaction patterns, uncertainty communication, and trust design. Even one AI-adjacent case study or a thoughtful LinkedIn post about AI UX challenges can open doors here.

Reliable option

Digital agencies and design studios

Agencies provide a reliable bridge during a longer search — diverse project exposure, faster hiring, and occasionally convert to full-time opportunities at client companies. Rates for freelance UX work at agencies run $60–$120/hour.

Honest reality

UX hiring is more competitive than engineering right now

UX design hiring contracted in 2023–2024 along with tech layoffs. The market is recovering but remains competitive — particularly at mid-level where the ratio of applicants to roles is highest. Portfolio quality is the primary differentiator.

90-day plan

Your roadmap to
next offer.

1
Week 1–2

Portfolio audited, feedback received, LinkedIn updated

Portfolio reviewed with fresh eyes. Impact metrics added to all case studies. Portfolio feedback received from at least one working designer. LinkedIn updated with portfolio link prominently. Leapr analysis done. Don't start mass applying until your portfolio is genuinely competitive — you only get one first impression per company.

2
Week 2–5

Apply to companies in your domain vertical first

Your domain expertise is your strongest differentiator in a competitive market. Apply to companies in your previous industry first — your knowledge of their users and constraints makes you immediately more credible. 10–15 targeted applications per week with a personalised note highlighting your relevant domain experience.

3
Week 3–6

Prepare your portfolio presentation rigorously

UX interviews always include a portfolio walkthrough. Practice presenting your strongest case study out loud — ideally with a timer and to another person. The goal: in 15 minutes, explain the problem, your research, your key decisions and why, what you shipped, and what you'd do differently. Interviewers evaluate your thinking process as much as the designs themselves.

4
Week 4–8

Build parallel processes and consider freelance bridges

Keep 4–5 active processes running. UX searches take 7–14 weeks on average — running sequentially means spending 6 months on a process that could be 3. If your pipeline is thin at week 4, add freelance applications to design agencies and platforms as a parallel income track.

5
Week 8–14

Negotiate your full package including design team quality

The quality of the design team and the maturity of the design practice at your target company affects your career growth significantly more than a 5–10% salary difference. Ask in interviews: "How does design influence product decisions here?" and "What does the design review process look like?" Evaluate the answers carefully before accepting an offer.

What not to do

Mistakes that
cost you weeks.

Applying with a portfolio that only shows final screens

Portfolios showing polished final designs without the research, decisions, and process behind them are skipped by experienced hiring managers. Every case study must show: what you learned from users, how that shaped your decisions, the iterations you made, and what changed because of your work.

Mass applying before your portfolio is competitive

Applying to 100 companies with a weak portfolio is worse than applying to 20 companies with a strong one. You'll get screened out before a human sees your work, and many of those companies won't consider you again for 6–12 months. Get your portfolio right first.

Only applying to companies you admire without considering your domain fit

A designer with 4 years in healthcare applying to a consumer gaming company is competing on pure design skill against designers who understand gaming users deeply. Apply to companies in your domain vertical first — your contextual knowledge is a genuine competitive advantage.

Underselling your research skills

Many UX designers present themselves as visual designers who also do some research. In a competitive market, designers who lead with research depth — who can facilitate discovery, synthesise findings, and demonstrate how research shaped shipped product — stand out. Lead with your thinking, not your Figma skills.
