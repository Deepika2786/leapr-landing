First 7 days

What to do
this week.

Cybersecurity roles fill faster than almost any other tech discipline when candidates are positioned correctly. The first week is about getting visible to the right people, not perfecting your resume.

Day 1–2

File for unemployment and clear your financial picture

Do this before anything else. Most states have a waiting period — every day you delay costs you money. Calculate your monthly burn rate. Knowing your exact runway removes panic from your job search and lets you make better decisions.

Day 2–3

Update LinkedIn and turn on Open to Work

Security recruiters search LinkedIn daily. Your headline should lead with your strongest credential — "SOC Analyst | SIEM | CompTIA Security+ | Incident Response." Being findable is worth more than a perfect resume in week one.

Day 3–5

Apply directly to 3–5 MSSPs immediately

Managed Security Service Providers (CrowdStrike, Arctic Wolf, Secureworks, Rapid7) hire continuously. They're the most reliable volume hirers in security and actively recruit analysts regardless of market conditions. Apply this week, not after you've "finished polishing your resume."

Day 5–7

Activate your security community network

Message former SOC colleagues, security managers, and contacts from security conferences or online communities (SANS, ISC2, local BSides chapters). Security is a tight-knit community — referrals close significantly faster than cold applications in this field.

Your assets

What you have that
the market wants.

The security talent shortage means your skills are in higher demand than you probably feel right now. Companies that laid off security staff often discover within months that they cut too deep — and rehire.

Extremely scarce

SIEM experience — Splunk or Sentinel

Hands-on Splunk or Microsoft Sentinel experience is one of the most searched skills in security job listings. If you have it, it belongs in your headline, not buried in your skills section.

High demand

Incident response and threat hunting

Analysts who can describe real IR scenarios they've managed — specific attack types, containment actions, post-incident documentation — stand out immediately in interviews. Prepare 2–3 specific stories.

Certification premium

CompTIA Security+, CySA+, CISSP

Security+ is the baseline that passes HR screening. CySA+ signals analytical depth. CISSP signals seniority. If yours are current, lead with them. If they're lapsing, renew before your resume goes out.

Growing fast

Cloud security experience

AWS Security Specialty, Microsoft SC-200, and general cloud security experience command significant premiums over on-premise security skills. If you have any cloud security exposure, surface it prominently.

Highest volume hiring

MSSPs and security consultancies

CrowdStrike, Arctic Wolf, Secureworks, Palo Alto, Rapid7, and dozens of regional MSSPs are the most reliable security employers. They hire at scale and are less affected by tech sector layoff cycles.

Strong demand

Financial services and healthcare

Both sectors are under heavy regulatory pressure and hire security analysts continuously. They tend to pay above market and offer more stability than pure tech companies.

Clearance premium

Government and defence

If you hold or can obtain a security clearance, government and defence contractor roles pay significant premiums and have near-zero unemployment. Worth exploring if you haven't already.

Remote availability

Moderate remote options

Security roles are less remote-friendly than cloud engineering — many require on-site presence for compliance reasons. Hybrid is common. Factor this into your geographic search radius.

90-day plan

Your roadmap to
next offer.

1
Week 1–2

Get visible and apply to MSSPs immediately

LinkedIn updated. 5 MSSP applications submitted. 15 network messages sent. Leapr analysis done. Your certifications checked for expiry. This is the week that determines how long your search takes.

2
Week 2–5

Apply strategically with tailored applications

15–20 targeted applications per week. For each role, mirror the job description's specific tool names — "Splunk" not "SIEM tools," "CrowdStrike Falcon" not "EDR platform." ATS systems keyword-match before humans see your resume.

3
Week 3–6

Prepare your IR stories for interviews

Security interviews always ask you to walk through a real incident. Prepare 3 specific stories using STAR format: an alert you triaged, an incident you contained, a threat hunt you ran. Specificity — attack type, tools used, timeline, outcome — is what moves you from "candidate" to "offer."

4
Week 4–8

Build parallel processes and create leverage

Keep 5–8 active interview processes running simultaneously. When one reaches offer stage, use it to accelerate the others. Never evaluate offers in isolation — always know what else is in your pipeline.

5
Week 6–12

Negotiate — security analysts undersell themselves

The security talent shortage gives you more negotiating leverage than most candidates. Counter with a specific number. Know your total comp target including bonus and remote flexibility. Don't leave money on the table out of relief at receiving an offer.

What not to do

Mistakes that
cost you weeks.

Only applying to large enterprises

Large enterprises move slowly and often have rigid HR screening. MSSPs and mid-market companies hire faster, give you broader exposure, and are more likely to evaluate you on skills rather than credentials.

Applying with a generic resume to every posting

A resume that says "SIEM tools" when the job description says "Splunk" will fail ATS screening. 30 minutes tailoring your resume per application beats 5 minutes on 10 applications.

Waiting to update certifications until after landing

Lapsed certifications create doubt in a field where credentials signal currency. Check expiry dates on day one and renew before your resume goes anywhere.

Overlooking contract and consulting opportunities

Security contract rates are strong ($60–$120/hour depending on specialisation). Short contracts fill resume gaps, keep skills sharp, and occasionally convert to permanent roles.
