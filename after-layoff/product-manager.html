First 7 days

What to do
this week.

PM searches take longer than engineering searches on average — expect 8–16 weeks. The first week is about activating your network and getting clear on your positioning. Clarity on what you want and what you're good at cuts search time significantly.

Day 1–2

File unemployment and calculate your runway carefully

PM searches take longer. Knowing you have 5–6 months of runway lets you be selective rather than desperate. PMs who take the first offer out of financial pressure often find themselves in a company or domain that doesn't suit them — and search again within 18 months.

Day 2–3

Define your positioning before updating anything

Are you a B2B or B2C PM? Platform or growth? Enterprise or consumer? Technical or domain-expert? Trying to be everything makes you memorable to nobody. Your positioning should be one sentence: "I'm a B2B SaaS PM with 5 years in fintech, specialising in payments and compliance workflows."

Day 3–5

Update LinkedIn with impact statements, not responsibilities

"Managed roadmap" is forgettable. "Launched payments feature that increased checkout completion by 23%, contributing $4M ARR" is specific and memorable. Every bullet on your LinkedIn profile should answer "so what?" with a number. PMs who can quantify impact get interviews. Those who describe activities don't.

Day 5–7

Activate your PM network — former engineers, designers, and PMs

Former engineers and designers who've worked with you are your most credible advocates — they've seen you work cross-functionally. Message 15 specific people. Former managers are the most valuable — a manager referral often directly fast-tracks your application to a hiring manager interview.

Your assets

What you have that
the market wants.

Highest impact

Quantified shipped features

PMs who can say "I shipped X, it moved Y metric by Z%" are immediately more compelling than those who describe their responsibilities. Audit your career history and find the numbers — even rough ones. "~20% improvement" is better than no number at all.

Strong differentiator

Domain expertise in your vertical

A PM who understands fintech compliance, clinical workflows, or logistics constraints at a deep level is more valuable to companies in those verticals than a generalist PM. Position your domain expertise explicitly — it's your competitive advantage over candidates with similar PM experience but different backgrounds.

High demand right now

AI product experience

PMs who've shipped AI features — especially if they can speak to evaluation frameworks, user trust design, and model quality trade-offs — are in strong demand from AI-first companies. Even managing a team that shipped an AI feature counts if you can speak to the decisions made.

Valued at all levels

Cross-functional leadership evidence

PM interviews probe deeply on how you worked with engineering and design. Specific stories about navigating disagreement, reprioritising under pressure, and aligning stakeholders on a difficult decision are what move candidates from "good PM" to "offer."

Most active

Series B–D product companies

Growth-stage companies with 50–500 employees are the most active PM hirers. They give PMs real ownership and move faster than large companies. Target companies that have raised Series B or C in the last 12–18 months.

Active hiring

AI-first product companies

Companies building AI products are actively hiring PMs who can bridge product thinking and AI capability. Any AI product experience — even managing a team that shipped an LLM feature — is worth leading with when targeting these companies.

More selective

Large tech companies

FAANG and large tech PM roles are highly competitive — typically 500+ applicants per role. They move slowly and have extensive interview processes. Include them in your pipeline but don't rely on them as your primary track.

Honest reality

Remote is harder for PMs than engineers

55% of PM roles include remote options — lower than engineering disciplines. Many companies want PMs physically proximate to their engineering and design teams. Factor this into your geographic targeting more than you would for engineering roles.

90-day plan

Your roadmap to
next offer.

1
Week 1–2

Position yourself clearly and activate your network

One-sentence positioning defined. LinkedIn updated with impact statements. 15 personalised messages sent to former cross-functional colleagues and managers. Leapr analysis done so you know exactly where your profile is strongest.

2
Week 2–5

Apply to companies in your domain vertical first

Target companies in your previous domain first — your domain knowledge gives you a genuine advantage over PM candidates with similar experience but different backgrounds. 10–15 targeted applications per week. Each cover note (even 3 sentences) that explains your domain fit performs significantly better than a generic application.

3
Week 3–7

Prepare your interview portfolio intensively

PM interviews have three main components: behavioural (STAR format stories about specific situations), case studies (design a product for X, improve metric Y), and your portfolio walkthrough. Prepare 5 strong STAR stories, practice 10 case studies out loud with a timer, and refine how you tell the story of your most impactful shipped product. This preparation separates top candidates from average ones more than in any other interview format.

4
Week 5–10

Build 5+ parallel processes — PM searches require it

PM interview processes run 4–8 weeks. Never evaluate opportunities sequentially — you'll spend 6 months finishing processes one by one. Run 5+ simultaneously so offers converge in the same 2-week window, giving you real negotiating leverage.

5
Week 8–16

Negotiate on base, equity, and scope

The scope of what you own matters as much as compensation for career trajectory. A slightly lower-paying role with broader ownership is often better than a higher-paying role with a narrow slice of a large product. Negotiate both. Counter with a specific number on base — ranges consistently result in the lower end of the range.

What not to do

Mistakes that
cost you weeks.

Describing your responsibilities instead of your impact

Every PM manages a roadmap, runs sprints, and works cross-functionally. What makes you memorable is what you shipped and what it achieved. If every bullet on your resume describes what you did rather than what changed because of what you did, rewrite it entirely before sending another application.

Trying to appeal to every company instead of the right companies

A generic PM profile appeals to nobody. A "B2B fintech PM with payments and compliance depth" immediately resonates with every fintech company that needs exactly that. Positioning feels like narrowing — it's actually broadening your signal to the right audience.

Underpreparing for case study interviews

PM case studies are learnable and predictable. "Design a product for X," "how would you improve Y metric," "how do you prioritise between A and B" — these formats repeat. Candidates who've practiced 15+ cases out loud consistently outperform those who've read frameworks but never spoken them. Practice with a timer and ideally with a partner.

Applying only to large tech companies

FAANG PM processes take 3–5 months and have rejection rates above 95%. A portfolio of 5 large company applications alongside 40 scaleup and growth-stage applications is the right balance — not 50 large company applications while waiting for callbacks that rarely come.
